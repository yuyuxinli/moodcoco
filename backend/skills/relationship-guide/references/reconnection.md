## The core question underneath all disconnection

Sue Johnson's entire framework rests on this: beneath most conflict and distance lies an attachment question:

> **Are you Accessible? Responsive? Engaged?**

**Accessible**: Can I reach you? Are you open to me?
**Responsive**: Will you respond when I need you? Do my needs matter?
**Engaged**: Are you with me — emotionally present, not just physically here?

When we feel the answer is *no* — or *I'm not sure* — the attachment alarm fires. Everything that follows (the fight, the distance, the cycle) is an attempt to re-establish that yes.

The Hold Me Tight conversation is about asking and answering A.R.E. directly.

## Raw Spots (Conversation 2)

Raw spots are **attachment hypersensitivities** — places where you're unusually reactive because they connect to older, deeper hurt.

They're not character flaws. They're signals about what your attachment system has learned.

**Common raw spots:**
- Being dismissed or minimized → may connect to feeling invisible as a child
- Being criticized → may connect to shame wounds or a critical parent
- Being left or pulled away from → connects to abandonment experiences
- Not being seen as competent → connects to failures that felt defining
- Sexual rejection → may connect to feeling fundamentally undesirable

**How to identify your raw spot:**

Ask: "What's the thing my partner does that hits hardest — that goes from zero to overwhelming very fast?"
Then: "What does that remind me of? Where else in my life did I feel this?"

**Sharing your raw spots with your partner is deeply intimate.** It says: *Here's the place I'm most vulnerable. I trust you with it.*

Script for sharing:
> "I want to tell you about something that really gets to me. When you [action], something in me goes to [reaction] really fast. I think it's connected to [old experience / fear]. It's not really about you — it's about me. But I wanted you to know it's there."

## The Hold Me Tight Conversation (Conversation 4)

This is the core conversation — the one where you reach for each other directly, without the protectors running the show.

### Part 1: What am I most afraid of?

Go inward first. Beneath the pattern you've been in (pursue/withdraw, freeze/flee), beneath the specific fight:
> "What am I most afraid is true? About you? About me? About us?"

Common fears:
- "You don't really love me"
- "I'm too much for you"
- "You'll eventually leave"
- "I'm fundamentally alone"
- "I'm not really important to you"
- "I'm not enough to make you happy"

This is the exile speaking. These fears sound extreme in daylight. But they drive everything.

**IFS translation**: This is going to the exile beneath the protector. What is the exile's core fear or belief?

### Part 2: What do I need from you?

From your Self (not from the exile in panic, not from the protector in demand):
> "What I need — what I'm really asking for — is [reach]."

Examples:
- "I need to know you're not going anywhere"
- "I need to feel like I matter more than work / the phone / whatever"
- "I need you to tell me that you still want me"
- "I need to know that when I'm at my worst, you won't leave"
- "I need you to stay present even when I'm upset — not fix it, just stay"

### Part 3: The reach and the response

**The reach** (person A, the one going first):
Share the fear and the need from a place of vulnerability — not demand, not accusation. Self-led.

> "I know this might sound intense, but... I've been scared that I'm not really that important to you anymore. And I've been covering that up with [anger / distance / being busy]. What I actually need is to feel like I'm your person — that you choose me. Does that make sense?"

**The response** (person B):
This is not a moment for explaining your side. It's a moment to receive what was offered and respond to the attachment need directly.

> "You're my person. I want you to feel that. I know I haven't been showing up that way — I can see how that landed. You matter to me. You're not losing me."

This is what Johnson calls the "Hold Me Tight" moment — the direct reaching and being met. It doesn't require perfect words. It requires presence, honesty, and reaching *back*.

## What if one person can't reach yet?

Sometimes one partner isn't ready for this conversation — they're still in their protectors. That's okay. Don't push.

Instead:
> "I can see you're not ready for this right now. I'm not going anywhere. When you are, I'm here."

Or invite them through curiosity, not demand:
> "I wonder what's making it hard for you to come toward me right now. I'm not judging — I'm genuinely curious."

This is Self-to-Self invitation, not protector-vs-protector demand.

## Rebuilding when you've been disconnected a long time

Long-distance and freeze-flee patterns create distance that feels like the "natural state" of the relationship. It isn't — it's a defense structure.

Steps for rebuilding:
1. **Name it together**: "We've gotten very distant. I miss us. I think we've both been protecting ourselves."
2. **Small bids for connection** (Gottman): Turn toward each other in tiny moments — a look, a touch, showing interest in their day. Rebuilding happens micro-moment by micro-moment.
3. **Plan intentional connection**: Scheduled time together that isn't logistics, chores, or problem-solving. Just being together.
4. **Take the vulnerability risk** (Hold Me Tight conversation above): One person reaches. This is the turning point.

## Keeping Love Alive (Conversation 7)

Love is not a state — it's a *process* of continual reaching, losing connection briefly, and finding each other again.

The goal is not to eliminate disconnection but to:
- Catch disconnection earlier
- Repair faster
- Use repairs to deepen trust
- Know each other's raw spots and handle them with care
- Keep reaching for each other, deliberately

**The secure base**: When couples feel secure, they don't cling — they *venture*. Each can pursue their own life, take risks, grow, because they trust home is safe. The relationship as secure base makes both partners more alive, not less.

**Rituals of connection**: Brief but reliable. Morning check-in, end-of-day debrief, a way of parting and greeting. These micro-moments of intentional connection are the architecture of a secure bond.

## Connecting to IFS

Every Hold Me Tight conversation involves IFS work underneath:
- Getting the protectors to step back so the exile can speak
- Speaking FOR the exile (the vulnerable reach)
- Receiving the partner's response into the exile (the new experience of being met)

The exile that gets met — repeatedly — begins to trust differently. The protectors begin to relax. This is how long-term healing happens within a relationship.
