## Attachment injuries vs. ordinary hurt

Not all wounds are equal. An **attachment injury** is a specific kind of hurt that occurs at a moment when you desperately needed your partner and they:
- Were absent (physically or emotionally)
- Responded in a way that felt like abandonment, betrayal, or rejection
- Failed to protect you when you were vulnerable

These moments carry special weight because they confirm the attachment system's worst fears:
- "When it really mattered, you weren't there"
- "I can't trust you with the real me"
- "I'm ultimately alone"

Examples:
- Partner wasn't present (emotionally or physically) during a crisis, illness, loss
- Affair or breach of trust
- A moment of contempt or cruelty that cut deep
- Being abandoned in a moment of disclosed vulnerability ("I told you the worst thing about me and you used it against me")
- Major decisions made unilaterally that changed the shape of shared life

These injuries don't heal with normal repair processes because the wound keeps the attachment alarm raised. The protectors can't relax while the evidence of danger sits in memory.

## Why ordinary repair doesn't work

The injured partner cycles between wanting to forgive and being unable to. The injuring partner may feel they've apologized adequately, or may feel like they can never make it right.

What's happening (IFS):
- The injured partner's exile holds the memory of abandonment — and won't let go until it's witnessed and metabolized
- The protectors keep the exile defended ("I'm not going to get hurt like that again")
- The injuring partner's parts may be in shame (shut down) or defensiveness, preventing them from truly entering the injured partner's experience

What's happening (EFT):
- The attachment bond was damaged at a moment of maximum vulnerability
- Trust needs to be rebuilt not through words but through *new emotional experience*
- The injured partner needs a response from their partner that reaches the exile — acknowledgment, presence, and ownership that is felt, not just stated

## The Forgiveness Conversation (Hold Me Tight, Conversation 5)

### Step 1: The injured partner describes the injury

Not the facts — the *experience*. What it meant. What it did to them. What they felt and still feel.

The listener's job: Stay present. Don't defend. Don't explain. Receive it.

> "When [specific moment], I felt [abandoned/betrayed/alone]. What that did to me was [impact]. The part of me that trusted you completely — that part got really hurt. And I'm still carrying that."

### Step 2: The injuring partner responds from Self

This is the hardest part. The injuring partner needs to:
1. Acknowledge the specific impact (not just "I know I hurt you")
2. Enter their partner's experience with genuine empathy
3. Take full ownership without minimizing or explaining away
4. Express what it means to them that they caused this hurt

> "I hear you. When I [what they did], I wasn't there for you in the way you needed. I can only imagine what that felt like — to reach and find nothing. Or worse, to reach and get [what happened]. That was real. I'm so sorry. Not just sorry it happened — sorry for what I did and what it's done to you."

**What doesn't work:**
- "I'm sorry, but I was going through [my own stuff]" — context may be true but derails the empathy
- "I've apologized a hundred times" — this is a protector defending against the weight of the injury
- Generic apology without entering the specific experience

### Step 3: The injured partner can begin to access forgiveness

Forgiveness isn't forced or decided in a moment. It emerges when the exile feels:
- *Witnessed* — the injury is acknowledged as real and significant
- *Mattered* — their partner cared enough to actually enter the pain
- *Safe enough* — the partner has changed or is committed to changing

The injured partner can ask: "What I need to know is [attachment need — e.g., 'that you choose me', 'that it won't happen again', 'that you understand what it cost me']."

The injuring partner responds to this need directly.

## IFS perspective on forgiving

Forgiveness from an IFS lens isn't about the exile deciding to let go. It's about:

1. **Witnessing the exile's pain** — really going to the part that was hurt and seeing what it carries (the injury, the belief it formed, the fear it lives with now)

2. **The exile getting what it needed** — not from the past (which can't change), but from Self, or from a new experience with the partner. The exile's need is usually: *to not be alone in this anymore*

3. **The protectors relaxing** — once the exile feels cared for, the wall doesn't need to be as high

The injuring partner's work (IFS):
- Find the parts that are in shame or defensiveness
- Unblend enough to enter the injured partner's experience
- Let their Self speak directly to their partner's exile: "I see you. I caused that. I'm here now."

---

## What forgiveness is not

- **Not forgetting**: The injury remains in memory. Forgiveness doesn't erase it.
- **Not excusing**: Acknowledging someone's struggle doesn't make the harm acceptable.
- **Not immediate**: Especially for severe injuries, forgiveness is a gradual process. Pushing for it too fast re-injures.
- **Not reconciliation**: In cases of ongoing harm or abuse, forgiveness is possible without returning to the relationship.
- **Not a performance**: "I forgive you" said from a protector to end the discomfort is not forgiveness. The exile is still carrying it.

## When this is too much for the relationship alone

Some injuries are deep enough that working through them together — without facilitation — risks re-injury. Signs that professional support is needed:
- The conversation about the injury keeps re-activating into a fight
- One partner is too activated to receive or stay present
- The injury connects to trauma (abuse, assault, major loss)
- Years have passed and the wound shows no signs of movement

An IFS-trained or EFT-trained couples therapist can facilitate the forgiveness conversation with containment that the couple can't provide for themselves mid-wound.
